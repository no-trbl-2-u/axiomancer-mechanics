# Character References — Personhood Prior Art Catalog

Organized by **personhood problem shape**. Each entry: Game | Pattern
name | What worked | What didn't | Player reception notes.

---

## 1. Voice-as-identity (the character is how they talk)

Personhood lives almost entirely in vocabulary, rhythm, and register.
No long bio is needed; one paragraph of dialogue establishes the
character.

| Game | Pattern | What worked | What didn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Disco Elysium** | Skill-voices as characters | Each of the 24 skills is a distinct voice with its own vocabulary, posture, and emotional register. Players described skills as their favourite characters — even though no skill ever appears on screen. | Reading load is enormous; players who skim miss the personhood entirely. The pattern only works if writing quality is sustained. |
| **Hades** | Iterated-revelation gods | Each Olympian has a signature speech pattern (Aphrodite purring, Zeus formal-pompous). Players recognise speakers without name tags. Voice consistency carries thousands of dialogue lines. | Writing-style locks can drift across DLC / expansion; players notice when Hephaestus suddenly "sounds different" from base game. |
| **Planescape: Torment (Morte)** | Mockery + memory | Morte's voice is wisecracking in the surface but uses very specific Sigil slang. Players quoted Morte's lines decades later. | Voice tightness requires writers who lock into one character; rotating writers break the voice. |

---

## 2. Witness-character (their judgment of the player is the point)

The character's primary function is to remember and react to what the
player has done. Their opinions are the world's verdict.

| Game | Pattern | What worked | What didn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pathologic 2 (Mark Immortell)** | Theatre director as authorial witness | A character literally narrates the player's mistakes back to them with theatrical framing. Players described it as the most cutting form of judgment. | Requires the world to be primarily judgmental; doesn't fit casts where most characters are warm. |
| **Undertale (Flowey)** | Save-state-aware mocker | Flowey remembers across save loads; mocks the player for reloading. Players celebrated it as the moment the game saw them. | Once the trick is known, repeatability suffers. Re-playthrough loses the punch. |
| **Disco Elysium (Kim Kitsuragi)** | Quiet professional witness | Kim notices everything, says less than half of it, but his silences become the player's barometer for "am I being a bad cop?" Praised as one of the best partner-NPCs ever written. | Subtlety means inattentive players miss the witness function entirely. |

---

## 3. Foil / mirror (defines the player by contrast)

The character exists to embody an opposite or amplified version of
something in the player. Their function is to make the player
articulate who they are.

| Game | Pattern | What worked | What didn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Planescape: Torment (Dak'kon)** | The follower who needs the player to believe | Dak'kon's mechanical strength depends on the player's certainty. Players described Dak'kon as the most philosophically resonant companion in any RPG. | Mechanical-belief coupling is hard to author; most attempts feel arbitrary. |
| **Mass Effect (Garrus / Wrex)** | The friend who shows you a darker version of yourself | Both characters mirror Shepard's tendency toward extrajudicial violence. Praised for letting the player feel their own ethics through someone else's hand. | If the mirror character is too similar, they collapse into a sidekick. If too different, the mirror function fails. |
| **Pathologic 2 (Daniil Dankovsky)** | The other lead with opposed methods | Played from one Healer's view, the other Healer is your sometimes-ally, sometimes-rival; the other game route plays them from inside. Praised as a Rashomon-style reveal of the same plague. | Requires the game to commit to the bifocal view; half-baked mirroring feels lazy. |

---

## 4. Secret-keeper (their personhood hinges on what they hide)

The character has a fact they know that the player doesn't. The
question is when (or if) the player learns it.

| Game | Pattern | What worked | What didn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Disco Elysium (Klaasje)** | The unreliable witness | Klaasje's testimony is partially untrue, and the player can detect the lies through stat checks. Players celebrated the slow-reveal arc as the highlight of Day 2. | Mystery characters lose impact on replay; on a third playthrough Klaasje is just a chess piece. |
| **Pathologic 2 (the Kin elders)** | Withheld cosmology | Several Kin elders refuse to explain their religion's metaphysics; the player learns only the parts they can earn. Praised for treating the player as not entitled to information. | Players who want exposition bounce off; the pattern requires trusting the audience to wait. |
| **Planescape: Torment (the Nameless One)** | The protagonist as secret-keeper | The player's own character is the secret. Memory recovery is the arc. Cited as the gold standard for narrative-as-amnesia. | The pattern works once. Cannot be repeated for supporting characters without feeling derivative. |

---

## 5. Mortality / fragility (characters who can die in the player's path)

The character is genuinely losable. Their existence is contingent.

| Game | Pattern | What worked | What didn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pathologic 2 (the Bound)** | Friends who can die between days | The player chooses which friends to save with limited medicine. Players described the mortality as the most painful design decision they had to make in any game. | Permadeath of NPCs requires authorial commitment to consequences; partial-death-rollbacks break the mechanic. |
| **Tyranny (Bleden Mark)** | Loyalty-gated companion death | Companions can leave or die based on edicts. Praised for making early choices have late-game faces. | Death-by-edict can feel arbitrary if the edict's logic isn't telegraphed. |
| **Mass Effect 2 (Suicide Mission)** | Final-mission permadeath | Every companion can die in the finale based on player decisions. Praised as the ME2 climax. | The all-or-nothing structure can make mid-game development feel like a placeholder for the finale. |

---

## How to use these references

When the user asks "what makes this character distinctive?", you
should be able to name 2–4 of the patterns above and articulate which
one fits. Pattern names are the vocabulary the user can borrow;
trade-offs are the friction they need to budget for.

If a pattern isn't here, mention that — adding new patterns to this
catalog is part of how the skill matures over time.
