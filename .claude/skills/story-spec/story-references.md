# Story References — Narrative Prior Art Catalog

Organized by **narrative problem shape**. Each entry: Game | Pattern name |
What worked | What didn't | Player reception notes.

---

## 1. State-reactive dialogue (no player choice; the world reacts)

World notices what the player has done. Dialogue changes based on flags, not
player selections. Writers author many lines; the runtime picks one.

| Game | Pattern | What worked | What didn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hades** | Chronotree / heat-reactive narration | Characters notice every major event; Zagreus's relationships deepen over dozens of runs without explicit choice. Players praised feeling genuinely known by the cast. | Zero player-choice means no sense of authorship. Dialogue doesn't change outcomes — it only reflects them. |
| **Disco Elysium** | Passive skill-voice thoughts | Skills comment on dialogue choices in real time, creating an inner monologue the player didn't "write" but still feels responsible for. Praised for layered identity. | Extremely high writing budget. Impossible to replicate at small scale without cutting scope severely. |
| **Pyre** | Relationship journal (Supergiant) | Each companion has a journal that updates after each ritual. Players felt attachment through accumulated small moments, not dramatic choices. | Requires tight pacing control; slow-burning arcs can feel invisible until mid-game. |

---

## 2. Player-choice branching (choices alter outcomes or moral state)

Player selects responses; selections have mechanical or narrative consequences.

| Game | Pattern | What worked | What didn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Disco Elysium** | Skill-gated dialogue options | Options are locked/unlocked by stats, not morality explicitly. Players felt authorship without feeling judged — the gate was competence, not virtue. | Requires a rich stat system. The fiction of "I'm making a choice" collapses if the player reloads for locked options. |
| **Undertale** | ACT system (spare/fight branching) | Every enemy is an NPC with a unique resolution path. Players celebrated discovering each creature's non-lethal resolution. Full run-type (pacifist/genocide) branches the whole game. | Binary outcomes (spare vs. kill) can feel arbitrary until the logic clicks. Genocide route was so dark some players felt it was punishment for curiosity. |
| **Tyranny** | Fatebinder edicts | Player's opening choices in the prologue permanently alter world state before play begins. Praised for making every run feel narratively distinct. | Consequence weight was front-loaded; mid-game choices felt less permanent. Some players bounced off irreversibility early. |
| **Planescape: Torment** | "What can change the nature of a man?" | Philosophical questions with no right answer. Player stats change how the question lands, not whether you can ask it. Widely praised for treating the player as an adult. | Dense text barrier; not universally accessible. The mechanic only works if writing quality is exceptional throughout. |

## 3. Recurring companion arcs (relationship meters, unlocks through repetition)

Relationships deepen over multiple encounters, not single conversations.

| Game | Pattern | What worked | What didn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hades** | Nectar/Ambrosia gift system | Giving items unlocks deeper dialogue over many runs. Players described relationships as genuinely earned rather than clicked through. | Unlock gates can feel arbitrary if the player doesn't read the implied pacing. Ambrosia feels like a paywall on story. |
| **Fire Emblem: Three Houses** | Tea time + support system | One-on-one scenes unlock over time; tea time adds a mini-game layer. Players loved the intimacy of small interactions. | Support conversations can become rote; late-tier supports feel obligatory rather than surprising. |
| **Pathologic 2** | Townspeople memory | NPCs remember whether the player visited them, helped them, or let them die. Praised for making the town feel alive and consequential. | Extremely punishing; players who couldn't keep up felt their relationships collapsed for unclear reasons. |

---

## 4. Voice and personality differentiation across NPCs

How different characters feel distinct even when discussing the same topic.

| Game | Pattern | What worked | What didn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hades** | Each deity has a distinct speech register | Dionysus speaks in plurals ("We think..."), Thanatos is clipped and formal, Hypnos is casual and slightly confused. Players recognize characters by voice alone. | High upfront voice-design cost; inconsistencies creep in over hundreds of lines without rigorous review. |
| **Pentiment** | Historical register variation | NPCs in 16th-century Bavaria speak in registers appropriate to class and education. Players praised immersion; the language itself was characterization. | Inaccessible if players don't already have cultural context. Historical accuracy created a learnability barrier. |
| **Disco Elysium** | Internal skill monologue voices | Each skill has a distinct personality in the thought cabinet. Electrochemistry is reckless, Empathy is gentle, Rhetoric is pompous. Players found the inner cast more vivid than most RPG companions. | Requires extraordinary writing density; individual skill voices risk inconsistency across a large team. |

---

## 5. Moral judgment systems (NPCs who remember and react to past actions)

The world evaluates the player's moral record and responds — not through a meter, but through NPC dialogue and behavior.

| Game | Pattern | What worked | What didn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Undertale** | Genocide route monster memories | In the genocide route, monsters you've killed are referenced by survivors. The Sans final battle is explicitly about the player's meta-record. Players described it as genuinely disturbing. | Works only in a context where the player already cares about the characters; cold start players don't feel the weight. |
| **Pathologic 2** | Bachelor's final judgment | NPCs deliver a verdict on your choices in the ending sequence. Players praised it as earned; some found it too on-the-nose. | Verdict framing can feel like the game is scoring you rather than the characters reacting. |
| **Tyranny** | Loyalty/fear companion meters | Companions track whether actions built loyalty or fear. Both are valid; the game doesn't moralize. Players appreciated the ambiguity. | Some players found the binary axes limiting; the system couldn't represent "complicated respect." |
| **Disco Elysium** | Political alignment via thought cabinet | Equipping thoughts shifts the detective's ideology; NPCs respond to the resulting persona. Players celebrated building a coherent political identity. | Alignment drift can feel random if players equip/unequip thoughts without reading carefully. |
