# World References — Location & Atmosphere Prior Art Catalog

Organized by **placeness problem shape**. Each entry: Game | Pattern
name | What worked | What didn't | Player reception notes.

---

## 1. Place-as-character (the location has a voice)

The location is treated like an NPC with personality, mood, and
reactions. Atmosphere does the work dialogue would do elsewhere.

| Game | Pattern | What worked | What didn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Disco Elysium** | Skill-narrated environment | The world talks back through internalised skill voices (Inland Empire describing curtains, Conceptualisation framing graffiti). Praised for making every doorway dense with character. | Authoring cost is enormous; one room can carry 20+ pages of conditional text. Re-readability suffers if the player re-enters often. |
| **Pathologic 2** | Town-as-organism | The town of Gorkhon is described with anatomical language; districts are organs, the player is a disease vector. Praised for making "place" feel alive and resentful. | Extremely punishing — players who don't read the atmosphere die. Bounce rate from new players is high. |
| **Sunless Sea** | Port-as-personality | Each port has a fixed voice and one or two iconic phrases ("Visit London. Visit London. Visit London."). Players described ports as characters they remembered without faces. | Voice consistency depends on a tight pool of writers; expansion content sometimes broke the voice lock. |

---

## 2. Place-as-puzzle (the location is the obstacle)

The geometry, state, or rules of the place are what the player is
solving. NPCs are scenery.

| Game | Pattern | What worked | What didn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Outer Wilds** | Time-loop locations with internal state | Each location is a knowledge-puzzle that resets at the 22-minute mark; the player's understanding is the only persistent state. Players described moments of insight as the best in any game. | Knowledge-only progression means the player can softlock by reading guides. The fiction relies on first-discovery surprise. |
| **Hollow Knight** | Layered map with progression keys | Each region is initially impassable in some dimension (jumps, double jumps, dives). Re-entry is the reward. Players celebrated the moment they realised they could now reach a previously blocked area. | Backtracking can feel like content padding without strong reasons to revisit. |
| **Dark Souls (Lordran)** | Interconnected vertical map | The world is one continuous puzzle of shortcuts; ascending and descending Lordran is the meta-quest. Praised for making the geography itself feel earned. | Bonfires-as-checkpoints constrain pacing; if a region has no bonfire, exploration becomes punishing rather than tense. |

---

## 3. Place-as-state (the location changes with progression)

The same node visited at different points in the story is a different
location — flags, NPCs, atmosphere all shift.

| Game | Pattern | What worked | What didn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pathologic 2** | Day-by-day district decay | Every district visibly worsens over the 12-day arc. Players praised the sense of a world dying under their hands. | Some districts decay invisibly until the player passes through; can feel arbitrary if the player misses the "before" state. |
| **Undertale** | Reactive geography to run-type | Identical rooms have different occupants, different prose, different threats depending on pacifist / neutral / genocide path. Players loved finding small differences on replays. | Authoring multiplier is real — each room is effectively 3–4 versions. |
| **Tyranny** | Edict-altered regions | A region's faction control flips based on the player's edicts; the same town has different shops, different NPC postures. Praised for making early choices feel weighty. | Authoring tax compounds — every region needs alt-state content even if most players never see it. |

---

## 4. Hostile architecture (the place fights the player)

The location applies mechanical pressure — DoT, attribute drain,
restricted actions — distinct from any enemy.

| Game | Pattern | What worked | What didn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mörk Borg** | Doomsday calendar zones | Some regions advance the apocalypse clock; standing still costs. Praised for making movement itself feel meaningful. | Calendar pressure can collapse into "just rush" if the player isn't given reason to linger. |
| **Pathologic 2** | Plague clouds + hunger + sleep | Multiple environmental meters drain concurrently; the player chooses which to neglect. Players described it as "every choice is a triage." | Stacking meters can feel like spreadsheet management for players who wanted narrative. |
| **Dark Souls (Blighttown)** | Toxic environmental DoT | A persistent poison effect makes Blighttown nearly impossible to traverse without curing items or specific resistances. Cemented as the most-remembered zone in the game. | Hated by a sizable fraction of players; "I won't go back to Blighttown" is a meme. |

---

## 5. Optional / hidden locations (off-map weight)

Locations that exist but are not signposted. The world is bigger than
the critical path suggests.

| Game | Pattern | What worked | What didn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Dark Souls (Painted World, Ash Lake)** | Optional sub-worlds | Players described stumbling into Ash Lake as the most awe-inspiring moment of their playthrough. The optional-ness is the magic. | Bounce: many players never find these locations and don't know what they missed. |
| **Hollow Knight (White Palace, Path of Pain)** | Skill-gated optional zone | A late-game optional zone that requires platforming mastery. Praised for "earned" content. | Skill-gating optional content can feel like punishment to players who can't clear it. |
| **Sunless Sea (Avid Horizon)** | End-of-map sublime | A location at the edge of the map that effectively ends the game when you reach it. Players described it as cinematic and well-earned. | If signposted too early it loses the punch; if signposted too late, players never see it. |

---

## How to use these references

When the user asks "how should this location feel?", you should be
able to name 2–4 of the patterns above and articulate which one fits.
Pattern names are the vocabulary the user can borrow; trade-offs are
the friction they need to budget for.

If a pattern isn't here, mention that — adding new patterns to this
catalog is part of how the skill matures over time.
